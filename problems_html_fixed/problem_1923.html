Problem 1923 - 2020 HMMT Team P9 of 10, N

Difficulty: 7

Let \(p > 5\) be a prime number. Show that there exists a prime number \(q < p\) and a positive integer \(n\) such that \(p\) divides \(n^2-q\).

Thinking

Okay, so we basically want to show that there exists some prime \(< p\) that is a QR mod \(p\). Suppose FTSOC that there doesn't. Then note that the sum of the exponents of primes in the factorization of \(p + 1\), which is a QR, must be even, since all the primes there are \(< p\), and thus, NQRs. Similarly, the sum of exponents in the factorization of \(p + 4\), \(p + 6\), \(p+9\), etc. \( < 2p\) must be even (unless they are prime). I'm trying to think of a counting-esque way (since there are \((p-1)/2\) QRs and NQRs) to disprove this but it doesn't seem to be very straightforward, since products of two distinct prime products can be equal.

Here I took a hint after being stuck for a bit: quadratic reciprocity (which means it was just a tech skill issue on my part). Quadratic reciprocity kills the problem, since it says that for an odd prime \(q < p\), \(p\) is an NQR if and only if \[(-1)^{(p-1)(q-1)/4} = 1\] So we can now divide into \(2\) cases: \(p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\) and \(p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}\).

When \(p \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\), then \(p\) is an NQR for all odd primes \(q < p\). Now looking at \(p - 1\), if an odd prime divides it, then \(p\) is a QR modulo that prime, so \(p = 2^n + 1\), which means \(p = 2^{2^m} + 1\). But now \(2\) is a quadratic residue modulo \(p\) since \[2^{2^{2^m - 1}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{2^m} + 1}\] which is a contradiction.

Now suppose \(p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}\), in which case \(p\) is an NQR for all primes \(q \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\) and a QR for all primes \(q \equiv 3 \pmod{4}\). We could look at \(p - 1\) and say things about the \(3 \pmod{4}\) factors, but instead of dealing with the factors of \(2\), let's just look at \(p - 6\). Since \(6 = 2\times 3\) is a QR mod \(p\), the only primes that divide it must be \(3 \pmod{4}\) primes. Since \(p - 6 \equiv 3 - 6 \equiv 1 \pmod{4}\), there must be an even number of primes dividing \(p - 6\). However, since all these primes are NQRs mod \(p\), we obtain that \(p - 6\) is a QR mod \(p\). But since \(p \equiv 3 \pmod{4}\) where \(-1\) is an NQR, \[p - 6 \equiv -6 \equiv 2 \times 3 \times (-1) = \text{NQR} \times \text{NQR} \times \text{NQR} = \text{NQR}\] which is a contradiction.
